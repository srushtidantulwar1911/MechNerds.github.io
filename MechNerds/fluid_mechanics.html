Fluid Mechanics


1. Continuity Equation

The continuity equation is a statement of mass conservation for a fluid flowing through a conduit. It states that the mass flow rate (m˙) at any point within an incompressible flow system remains constant.
Continuity Equation: ˙m= ρAV
Where: ˙m = Mass flow rate (kg/s)
ρ = Density of the fluid (kg/m³)
A = Cross-sectional area of the flow (m²)
V = Velocity of the fluid (m/s)

2. Bernoulli's Equation

Bernoulli's equation is a fundamental principle in fluid mechanics that describes the relationship between pressure, velocity, and potential energy of a fluid along a streamline.
It is based on the conservation of energy for an incompressible, steady flow.
Bernoulli's Equation: P+1/2 ρV^2+ρgh=constant
Where: P = Pressure of the fluid (Pa)
ρ = Density of the fluid (kg/m³)
V = Velocity of the fluid (m/s)
g = Acceleration due to gravity (m/s²)
ℎ = Height above a reference level (m)

3. Navier-Stokes Equations

The Navier-Stokes equations describe the motion of fluid substances and are a set of partial differential equations.
They account for the conservation of mass, momentum, and energy and are essential for solving complex fluid flow problems.

4. Reynolds Number (Re)

The Reynolds number is a dimensionless quantity used to predict the flow regime (laminar, transitional, or turbulent) of a fluid in a given situation.
It is defined as the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces.
Reynolds Number: Re=ρVL/μ
Where: ρ = Density of the fluid (kg/m³)
V = Velocity of the fluid (m/s)
L = Characteristic length (m)
μ = Dynamic viscosity of the fluid (Pa·s)

5. Pressure Drop in Pipes (Darcy-Weisbach Equation)

The Darcy-Weisbach equation is commonly used to calculate the pressure drop (ΔP) in pipes due to friction.
It relates the pressure drop to the pipe length, flow rate, diameter, and friction factor.
Darcy-Weisbach Equation: ΔP= 4fLρV^2/ 2D
f = Darcy friction factor
L = Pipe length (m)
ρ = Density of the fluid (kg/m³)
V = Fluid velocity (m/s)
D = Pipe diameter (m)
