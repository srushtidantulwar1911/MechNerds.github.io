Heat Transfer


1. Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction

Fourier's law describes heat conduction in a solid material.
It states that the rate of heat transfer through a material is directly proportional to the temperature gradient and the cross-sectional area, and inversely proportional to the material's thermal conductivity.
Fourier's Law Formula: ˙Q= −kAdx/dT
Where: ​ ˙Q = Heat transfer rate (W or J/s)
k = Thermal conductivity of the material (W/(m·K))
A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
dx/dT= Temperature gradient (K/m)

2. Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton's law of cooling describes the rate of convective heat transfer between a solid surface and a surrounding fluid (usually air or water).
It states that the rate of heat loss or gain is proportional to the temperature difference between the surface and the fluid.
Newton's Law of Cooling Formula: ˙Q= hA(Ts−Tf)
​ ˙Q = Heat transfer rate (W or J/s)
ℎ = Convective heat transfer coefficient (W/(m²·K))
A = Surface area (m²)
Ts= Surface temperature (K)
Tf = Fluid temperature (K)

3. Stefan-Boltzmann Law

The Stefan-Boltzmann law describes the total power radiated by a black body (a perfect emitter and absorber of radiation) per unit area.
It states that the radiated power is proportional to the fourth power of the absolute temperature.
Stefan-Boltzmann Law Formula: ˙Q = σA(T4−Tsur^4)
​ ˙Q = Radiated power (W or J/s)
σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67×10−8 W/(m²·K⁴))
A = Surface area (m²)
T = Absolute temperature of the radiating body (K) ​
Tsur = Absolute temperature of the surrounding medium (K)

4. Heat Conduction Equation

The heat conduction equation is a partial differential equation that governs heat conduction in a solid.
It describes how temperature changes with time and position in a material subjected to heat conduction.
Heat Conduction Equation:
∂t/∂T=α ∂^2/∂x^2
Where: T = Temperature (K)
t = Time (s)
x = Spatial position (m)
α = Thermal diffusivity (ρc pk) where
κ is the thermal conductivity (W/(m·K)),
ρ is the density (kg/m³), and is the specific heat capacity (J/(kg·K)).
